# Third Gender and Beyond: Non-Binary Gender Systems

---

## Beyond the Binary

**Western Assumption:**
- Two genders (male/female)
- Fixed, natural, universal
- Corresponding to two sexes

**Anthropological Discovery:**
- Many societies have more than two genders
- Third, fourth, fifth genders
- Fluid, contextual genders
- Challenges Western binary

**Not "Transgender":**
- Third genders ≠ Western transgender
- Culturally specific categories
- Own meanings, roles, histories
- Can't impose Western terms

## Native American Two-Spirit

### Historical Terms and Colonialism

**"Berdache":**
- French term (originally Persian)
- Used by anthropologists
- Now rejected (colonial, offensive)

**"Two-Spirit":**
- Coined 1990 (Winnipeg conference)
- Indigenous people chose
- Reclaim tradition
- Pan-Indian

**Diversity:**
- Hundreds of Native nations
- Different terms, roles, meanings
- Can't generalize

### Traditional Roles

**Multiple Gender Traditions:**

> **Navajo (Diné) - Nádleehí**
>
> **Four Genders:**
> - Asdzáán (woman)
> - Hastiin (man)
> - Nádleehí (masculine female-bodied)
> - Nádleehí (feminine male-bodied)
>
> **Roles:**
> - Respected, special
> - Often healers, artists
> - Mediate between genders
> - Ceremonial roles

> **Lakota - Winkté**
>
> - Male-bodied, feminine
> - Vision-given role
> - Sacred
> - Keepers of traditions
> - Artistic, craftwork
> - Sometimes married men

> **Zuni - Lhamana**
>
> - We'wha (famous)
> - Male-bodied, women's work
> - Weaving, pottery
> - Mediators
> - Special spiritual power

**Common Patterns:**
- Spiritual significance
- Special roles (ceremonial, artistic)
- Mediate between genders
- Respected (generally)

**Matilda Coxe Stevenson:**
- We'wha (1890s)
- Zuni lhamana
- Visited Washington DC
- Not recognized as male-bodied
- Complexity of gender

### Colonial Suppression

**European Conquest:**
- Horrified by gender variance
- "Sodomy"
- Forced conversion, suppression
- Boarding schools erased

**Contemporary Revival:**
- Two-Spirit identity
- Reclaim traditions
- Pride, visibility
- Political, spiritual

**Debates:**
- Pan-Indian vs. nation-specific
- Appropriation by non-Natives
- Modern vs. traditional

## South Asian Hijra

### Who Are Hijra?

**India, Pakistan, Bangladesh:**
- Third gender
- Neither man nor woman
- Ancient tradition (millennia)

**Categories:**
- Born intersex
- Male-bodied, feminine identity
- Voluntary castration (some)
- Receive goddess Bahuchara Mata

**Community:**
- Organized in houses (households)
- Guru-chela (teacher-disciple)
- Lineages
- Khusra (Pakistan/Bangladesh)

### Social Roles

**Traditional:**
- Bless births, weddings
- Performances (dancing, singing)
- Badhai (collect payment)
- Feared/respected (can curse)

**Religious:**
- Devotees of Bahuchara Mata
- Also Allah, other deities
- Temples, shrines
- Ritual emasculation

**Contemporary:**
- Marginalized, stigma
- Sex work (survival)
- Begging
- Discrimination

### Serena Nanda: "Neither Man Nor Woman" (1990)

**Ethnography:**
- Detailed study
- Lives, rituals, community
- Complexity

**Not "Transgender":**
- Culturally specific
- Spiritual, social category
- Not Western identity

**Hijra Identity:**
- "Not man"
- "Not woman"
- Hijra (distinct category)

### Legal Recognition

**India (2014):**
- Supreme Court recognized third gender
- Legal rights
- Passports, documents
- Progress, but discrimination persists

**Pakistan (2009):**
- Third gender on ID cards
- Rights

**Bangladesh:**
- Recognition

**Challenges:**
- Stigma continues
- Violence
- Economic marginalization

## Pacific Gender Diversity

### Samoa - Fa'afafine

**"In the manner of a woman":**
- Male-bodied
- Feminine gender role
- Culturally recognized
- Not stigmatized (traditionally)

**Roles:**
- Help family (esp. if few daughters)
- Household work
- Caregiving elders
- Respected

**Not "Gay":**
- Separate category
- Men who partner with fa'afafine not "gay"
- Gender, not sexuality
- Western categories don't fit

**Contemporary:**
- Visibility, acceptance
- Some adopt Western "transgender"
- Others maintain fa'afafine identity
- Fa'afafine Association (advocacy)

## Indonesia - Waria and Bugis Five Genders

### Waria (Indonesia)

**Wanita (woman) + Pria (man):**
- Male-bodied, feminine
- Transgender identity
- Muslim-majority context

**Evelyn Blackwood, Tom Boellstorff:**
- Waria communities
- Islam and gender variance
- Negotiate identity

### Bugis (Sulawesi) - Five Genders

**Sharyn Graham Davies:**

**Five Genders:**
1. **Makkunrai** (cisgender women)
2. **Oroané** (cisgender men)
3. **Bissu** (androgynous, shamanic, sacred)
4. **Calabai** (male-bodied, feminine)
5. **Calalai** (female-bodied, masculine)

**Bissu:**
- Most sacred
- Priests, shamans
- Neither/both male and female
- Ritual specialists
- Nearly extinct (suppression)

**Insight:**
- Gender ≠ binary
- Multiple genders coexist
- Spiritual significance

## Southern Africa - "Women-Women Marriage"

### Nuer Woman-Woman Marriage (Evans-Pritchard)

**Female Husbands:**
- Barren woman "becomes man"
- Marries younger woman
- Younger woman has children (by male genitor)
- Female husband = pater (social father)
- Children belong to her

**Not Lesbianism:**
- No sexual relationship
- Social arrangement
- Ensures lineage continuity
- Gender as social role

### Nandi (Kenya) Female Husbands

**Similar:**
- Wealthy women marry women
- Ensure heirs
- Property, lineage

**Oboler Study:**
- Female husbands respected
- "Men" socially
- Gender = social position

## Albanian Sworn Virgins

### Balkan Gender Variance

**Albania, Montenegro:**
- Women take vow of virginity
- "Become men"
- Dress, live as men
- Patriarchal society

**Why?**
- Family has no male heir
- Avoid unwanted marriage
- Economic opportunity

**Antonia Young:**
- Ethnography
- Sworn virgins integrated as men
- Social gender transformation
- Chastity required

**Not Transgender (Western):**
- Strategic, social
- Patriarchy's terms
- Interesting case

## Theoretical Implications

### Challenging the Binary

**What Third Genders Teach:**
1. Gender ≠ biological sex
2. More than two possibilities
3. Culturally constructed
4. Spiritual, social meanings
5. Western binary not universal

### Gilbert Herdt: "Third Sex, Third Gender" (1994)

**Comparative Volume:**
- Global third gender practices
- Patterns and diversity
- Challenge universals

**Not All Same:**
- Can't lump together
- Culturally specific
- But: common challenge to binary

### Problems with "Third Gender" Category

**Critiques:**
- Reifies binary (one, two, THREE)
- Western analytical category
- Homogenizes diversity
- Better: multiple genders, fluidity

**Serena Nanda:**
- "Gender diversity" better than "third gender"
- Acknowledges variety

**Remember:** Gender diversity is global—many societies recognize more than two genders. Two-Spirit (Native American), hijra (South Asia), fa'afafine (Samoa), māhū (Hawai'i), bissu (Bugis), and many others challenge Western binary. These aren't "transgender" (Western category) but culturally specific third, fourth, fifth genders with own meanings, roles, histories. Often spiritual significance, special roles, mediate between genders. Colonial suppression, contemporary revival. Woman-woman marriage (Africa), sworn virgins (Albania), xanith (Oman) show gender as social position, not biology. Intersex shows biological sex isn't binary. Western transgender/non-binary challenge binary too, but different context. Understanding gender diversity reveals binary as cultural construct, not natural fact—humans create multiple ways of being gendered.
