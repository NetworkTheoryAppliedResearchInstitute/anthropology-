# Stone Tool Traditions: Technology Through Deep Time

---

## Stone Tools and Human Evolution

### Why Stone Tools Matter

**Preservation:**
- Stone survives (millions of years)
- Organic tools decay
- Archaeological record biased

**But:**
- Stone minority of toolkit
- Wood, bone, fiber more common (don't preserve)
- Ötzi: majority organic tools

**Information:**
- Technology level
- Cognitive abilities
- Subsistence strategies
- Cultural transmission
- Innovation, change over time

**Experimental Archaeology:**
- Replicate tools
- Understand techniques
- Don Crabtree, François Bordes pioneers
- Learn by doing

## Stone Tool Production Basics

### Lithic Technology

**Raw Materials:**
- Cryptocrystalline (fine-grained): flint, chert, obsidian
- Fracture predictably (conchoidal)
- Sharp edges

**Knapping:**
- Strike stone with hammerstone
- Controlled fracture
- Core (source stone) → flakes removed

**Types:**

**Hard Hammer:**
- Stone hammerstone
- Heavy blows
- Thick, robust flakes

**Soft Hammer:**
- Antler, wood, bone
- Controlled, precise
- Thinner flakes

**Pressure Flaking:**
- Press with antler/bone tool
- Fine retouch
- Shaping edges

**Terms:**
- **Core:** Stone from which flakes removed
- **Flake:** Piece struck off
- **Debitage:** Waste flakes
- **Retouch:** Secondary shaping

**Features:**
- Platform (where struck)
- Bulb of percussion (swelling)
- Conchoidal fracture (ripples)

## Mode 1: Oldowan (2.6-1.7 million years ago)

### Earliest Stone Tools

**Discovery:**
- Mary and Louis Leakey
- Olduvai Gorge, Tanzania (1960s)
- Named after site

**Age:**
- ~2.6 million years ago (earliest)
- Gona, Ethiopia (Sileshi Semaw)
- Lomekwi, Kenya (3.3 mya, debated—pre-Homo?)

**Makers:**
- Early Homo (habilis, rudolfensis)
- Maybe Australopithecus/Paranthropus? (debated)

**Technology:**
- Simple
- Hard hammer percussion
- Few flakes removed (3-5 typically)
- Irregular

**Tool Types:**

**Choppers:**
- Core with edge
- Heavy-duty cutting

**Flakes:**
- Sharp edges
- Cutting, scraping
- Actually more useful than cores

**Sonia Harmand:**
- Lomekwi 3 (Kenya)
- 3.3 million years
- Before Homo?
- Opportunistic pounding

**Function:**
- Butchery (cut marks on bones)
- Processing plants
- Bone marrow extraction (break bones)

**Cognitive Implications:**
- Intentional production
- Recognize sharp edges useful
- Foresight (make tools before need)
- But: simple, limited standardization

**Spread:**
- East Africa initially
- Homo erectus carries beyond Africa

## Mode 2: Acheulean (1.7 million-100,000 years ago)

### Hand Axes and Sophistication

**Discovery:**
- St. Acheul, France (19th century)
- Named after type site

**Age:**
- ~1.7 million years (Africa)
- Lasts over 1 million years!
- Most successful tool tradition ever

**Makers:**
- Homo erectus (primarily)
- Later: Homo heidelbergensis

**Technology:**
- Bifacial (flaked both sides)
- Symmetrical
- Large flake blanks or cores
- Soft hammer often

**Tool Types:**

**Hand Axes (Bifaces):**
- Teardrop or almond-shaped
- Carefully shaped
- Two faces
- Sharp edge around perimeter

**Cleavers:**
- Flat, sharp edge (like chisel)
- Chopping

**Picks:**
- Pointed, thick

**Function:**
- Debated!
- Butchery? (multi-purpose)
- Display? (some enormous, symmetrical)
- Thrown? (unlikely)

**The Puzzle:**
- Why so standardized?
- Why so long unchanged?
- Functional or symbolic?

**Cognitive Implications:**
- Mental template (envision final form)
- Symmetry (aesthetic sense?)
- Skill (hours to make)
- Teaching required
- Innovation slow

**Geographic Spread:**
- Africa, Europe, Asia
- **Movius Line:**
  - Harvey Movius (1940s)
  - East Asia lacks hand axes
  - Bamboo hypothesis (use bamboo instead)
  - Or: retained Oldowan longer

## Mode 3: Mousterian/Middle Paleolithic (300,000-30,000 years ago)

### Neanderthal Technology

**Named After:**
- Le Moustier, France

**Age:**
- ~300,000-30,000 years ago
- Middle Paleolithic (Europe)
- Middle Stone Age (Africa, different trajectory)

**Makers:**
- Neanderthals (Europe)
- Archaic Homo sapiens (Africa)

**Technology:**

**Levallois Technique:**
- **Prepared-core technology**
- Revolutionary!
- Shape core carefully
- Predetermine flake size/shape
- Strike → desired flake pops off
- One core → many flakes

**Process:**
1. Shape core (turtle shell form)
2. Prepare striking platform
3. Remove predetermined flake
4. Repeat (rejuvenate core)

**Advantages:**
- Efficient (more cutting edge per stone)
- Standardized flakes
- Predictable

**Tool Types:**
- Scrapers (hide processing)
- Points (spears)
- Knives
- Specialized forms

**François Bordes:**
- Classified Mousterian variants
- 60+ tool types
- Debated meaning (cultural? functional?)

**Hafting:**
- Attach stone to handles
- Birch tar (adhesive)
- Composite tools
- Planning, multi-step

**Cognitive Implications:**
- Foresight (prepared-core planning)
- Standardization
- Hafting (composite thinking)
- Neanderthals sophisticated
- Not "dumb brutes"

## Mode 4: Upper Paleolithic (50,000-10,000 years ago)

### Modern Human Revolution

**Age:**
- ~50,000-10,000 years ago
- Modern humans (Homo sapiens)

**Europe:**
- Replaced Neanderthals

**Technology:**

### Blade Technology

**Definition:**
- Blade: flake 2x longer than wide
- Parallel sides
- Very sharp

**Production:**
- Punch technique (indirect percussion)
- Antler punch placed on platform
- Strike punch (not core directly)
- Control, precision

**Advantages:**
- Maximum cutting edge per stone
- Efficiency peak
- Light, portable
- Standardized

**Core Types:**
- Prismatic (cylinder)
- Many blades removed (30+)

### Tool Types

**Burins:**
- Chisel-like point
- Engrave bone, antler, ivory
- Make other tools

**End Scrapers:**
- Blade with retouched end
- Hide processing

**Backed Blades:**
- One edge blunted (finger rest)
- Cutting edge sharp

**Microliths:**
- Tiny blades
- Composite tools (many set in handle)
- Arrows, sickles

**Projectile Points:**
- Elaborate (Solutrean laurel leaves)
- Artistic

**Regional Traditions:**

**Aurignacian** (43,000-28,000 BP):
- Earliest Upper Paleolithic (Europe)
- Modern humans arrive
- Blades, bone tools

**Gravettian** (33,000-22,000 BP):
- Shouldered points
- Venus figurines

**Solutrean** (22,000-17,000 BP):
- Exquisite pressure-flaked points
- Laurel leaves (masterpieces)

**Magdalenian** (17,000-12,000 BP):
- Bone, antler tools elaborate
- Harpoons
- Cave art peak (Lascaux, Altamira)

## Bone, Antler, and Ivory Tools

### Organic Technology

**Upper Paleolithic:**
- Explosion of organic tool use
- Bone needles (sewing, clothing)
- Harpoons, spear throwers (atlatl)
- Engravings, art objects

**Earlier:**
- Some Neanderthal bone tools
- But: Upper Paleolithic much more

**Examples:**
- Awls, punches
- Fishing gear
- Decorative items

**Preservation:**
- Rare except favorable conditions
- Probably underestimated

## Composite Tools

### Combining Materials

**Definition:**
- Multiple pieces
- Hafting (handles)
- Adhesives

**Advantages:**
- Replace broken parts
- Specialized components
- Efficiency

**Examples:**
- Spears (stone point + wooden shaft)
- Arrows
- Sickles (microliths in wooden handle)

**Cognitive:**
- Planning
- Multi-step process
- Different materials, techniques

## Regional Variation

### Africa's Different Path

**Middle Stone Age (Africa, ~300,000-30,000 years ago):**
- Contemporary with European Middle Paleolithic
- But: different
- Modern humans (Homo sapiens)

**Technology:**
- Points (hafted)
- Blades (earlier than Europe)
- Backed pieces
- Bone tools

**Later Stone Age (Africa, ~50,000-present):**
- Microliths
- Diverse regional traditions
- Pottery (early, ~10,000 BP)

**Blombos Cave (South Africa):**
- ~100,000-70,000 years ago
- Sophisticated tools
- Symbolic behavior (ochre, beads)
- **Earlier than European Upper Paleolithic**

**Lesson:**
- "Behavioral modernity" not single event
- Africa leads
- Multiple origins

**Arrival:**
- ~65,000 years ago (maritime crossing!)
- Sophisticated

**Technology:**
- Core and flake tools
- Ground-edge axes (earliest, ~50,000 BP)
- Regional variation

## Americas

**Arrival:**
- Debated (15,000+ years ago, maybe earlier)

**Clovis Culture (13,000-12,800 years ago):**
- Distinctive fluted points
- Widespread (North America)
- Big game hunting
- Pre-Clovis sites debated (Monte Verde, Chile)

**Folsom:**
- Successor to Clovis
- Bison hunters

## Microliths and the Mesolithic

### After the Ice Age

**Mesolithic (Europe, ~10,000-5,000 BCE):**
- "Middle Stone Age"
- After Paleolithic, before Neolithic
- Warm climate (forests)

**Technology:**
- Microliths (tiny blades)
- Composite tools (arrows, fish spears)
- Broad-spectrum diet (not just big game)

**Remember:** Stone tool traditions evolved over millions of years. **Mode 1 Oldowan** (2.6 mya earliest simple choppers/flakes Leaky Olduvai, Gona Ethiopia, early Homo/Australopithecus?, butchery cut marks). **Mode 2 Acheulean** (1.7 mya-100 kya hand axes bifacial symmetrical, Homo erectus/heidelbergensis, lasts 1+ million years most successful, function debated butchery or display, mental template symmetry skill teaching, Movius Line East Asia lacks bamboo hypothesis). **Mode 3 Mousterian Middle Paleolithic** (300-30 kya Neanderthals sophisticated, Levallois prepared-core revolutionary predetermine flake shape efficient, scrapers/points/knives specialized Bordes 60+ types, hafting birch tar composite tools, planning foresight not "dumb"). **Mode 4 Upper Paleolithic** (50-10 kya modern humans blade technology 2x longer parallel punch technique efficiency peak, burins/scrapers/microliths/projectile points, regional traditions Aurignacian/Gravettian/Solutrean laurel leaves/Magdalenian bone harpoons Lascaux/Altamira, bone/antler/ivory explosion). **Africa different**: Middle Stone Age contemporary but different (Blombos 100-70 kya sophisticated earlier than European Upper Paleolithic), Later Stone Age microliths. Australia 65 kya maritime crossing ground-edge axes 50 kya earliest. Americas Clovis 13 kya fluted points. Mesolithic 10-5 kya microliths composite tools. Understanding stone tool evolution reveals cognitive development—Oldowan simple intentional, Acheulean mental template symmetry unchanged, Mousterian planning Levallois, Upper Paleolithic blade efficiency innovation acceleration, regional variation not linear progress.
