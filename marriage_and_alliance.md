# Marriage and Alliance: Creating Social Bonds

---

## Marriage as Social Alliance

In many societies, marriage isn't primarily about love or individual happiness—it's about **creating alliances between families, lineages, or groups**.

**Claude Lévi-Strauss:** "Marriage is exchange."

**Not cynical—it's recognizing:**
- Marriage connects groups, not just individuals
- Creates ongoing relationships between families
- Involves transfer of goods, people, obligations
- Organizes society through systematic patterns

**Anthropology reveals:** Marriage rules and patterns show how societies create structure, prevent conflict, build cooperation.

---

## What Is Marriage?

### Defining Marriage (Anthropologically)

**Harder than it sounds!**

**Traditional definition problems:**
- "Union of man and woman" → excludes same-sex marriage
- "For procreation" → excludes many marriages (infertile, elderly, etc.)
- "Economic cooperation" → exists outside marriage too
- "Sexual exclusivity" → not universal

**Anthropological consensus:**
Marriage is a **culturally recognized relationship** that:
- Establishes rights and obligations
- Often (not always) involves sexuality and/or reproduction
- Creates relationships beyond the couple (affines = in-laws)
- May involve exchange of goods or services
- Typically has public recognition/ritual

**Varies enormously:** Forms, rules, meanings, purposes.

### Marriage vs. Mating

**Mating:** Biological reproduction

**Marriage:** Social institution organizing:
- Sexual rights
- Economic cooperation
- Child legitimacy
- Inheritance
- Political alliances

**Marriage socially organizes what biology leaves messy.**

## Marriage Rules: Exogamy and Endogamy

### Exogamy: Marry Out

**Rule:** Must marry outside your group.

**Levels:**
- Nuclear family (nearly universal incest taboo)
- Lineage/clan ("clan exogamy")
- Village
- Ethnic group

**Functions:**

**1. Prevent Incest:**
Close relatives typically prohibited (though definitions of "close" vary).

**2. Create Alliances:**
"Marry out or die out"—forces groups to ally.

**3. Expand Networks:**
Creates ties beyond immediate group.

> **Example: Trobriand Clans**
>
> Must marry outside your matrilineal clan:
> - Creates ongoing exchanges between clans
> - Kula exchanges follow marriage ties
> - Political alliances through marriage
> - Each marriage binds two clans

**Lineage Exogamy:**
Very common with unilineal descent—can't marry within patrilineage or matrilineage.

**Why?** 
- Group members like siblings
- Marry out to create external alliances
- Exchange partners with other groups

### Endogamy: Marry In

**Rule:** Must (or strongly prefer) marry within your group.

**Levels:**
- Caste (India)
- Class
- Ethnic group
- Religion
- Village/region

**Functions:**

**1. Maintain Group Boundaries:**
Keep wealth, power, purity within group.

**2. Preserve Status:**
Especially in stratified societies.

**3. Cultural Continuity:**
Shared religion, language, customs.

> **Example: Caste Endogamy (India)**
>
> Traditional rule: Marry within caste:
> - Maintain caste purity
> - Prevent pollution
> - Keep occupational groups separate
> - Violating endogamy = outcasting
>
> **Modern:** Legal prohibition of caste discrimination, but social pressure persists.

> **Example: Religious Endogamy**
>
> Many religions prefer/require co-religionists:
> - Jewish endogamy (maintain community)
> - Muslim preference for Muslim partners
> - Christian preferences historically strong
>
> **Modern:** More interfaith marriages, but still social pressure.

**Not Contradictory:**
Groups can be exogamous at one level, endogamous at another.
- Marry outside lineage (exogamy)
- But within ethnic group (endogamy)

## Cousin Marriage

### The Incest Taboo

**Nearly Universal:** Can't marry nuclear family (parents, siblings, children).

**But cousins?** Varies enormously.

### Types of Cousins (Review)

**Parallel Cousins:**
- Father's brother's children
- Mother's sister's children
- Same side of family, same-sex siblings

**Cross-Cousins:**
- Father's sister's children
- Mother's brother's children
- Different sides, opposite-sex siblings

### Cross-Cousin Marriage

**Preferred or Prescribed in many societies:**

**Logic (with exogamy):**
- Can't marry in own lineage
- Cross-cousins in different lineage → eligible
- Creates ongoing exchange between lineages
- Mother's brother gives wife to you, you give wife to his son

**Two Forms:**

**Matrilateral Cross-Cousin Marriage (MBD):**
- Marry mother's brother's daughter (MBD)
- Creates directional exchange: A→B→C→A (circle)
- Asymmetric (you receive from one group, give to another)

**Patrilateral Cross-Cousin Marriage (FZD):**
- Marry father's sister's daughter (FZD)
- Creates reciprocal exchange: A↔B (back-and-forth)
- Symmetric (groups exchange directly)

**Lévi-Strauss's Theory:**
- MBD marriage creates generalized exchange (complex alliance networks)
- FZD marriage creates restricted exchange (simple reciprocity)
- MBD more stable, creates larger alliances

> **Example: Traditional South India**
>
> MBD marriage preferred:
> - Ongoing relationship between lineages
> - Mother's brother important figure
> - Exchange partners across generations
> - Creates stable alliance networks

### Parallel Cousin Marriage

**Generally prohibited OR allowed/preferred (opposite patterns!):**

**Prohibited:**
- Where Iroquois terminology (parallel cousins = siblings)
- Would be like marrying sibling

**Preferred (FBD):**
- Some Middle Eastern societies (Arabic pattern)
- Father's brother's daughter = ideal wife
- Keeps property in patrilineage
- Strengthens patrilineal bonds
- Prevents fragmentation

> **Example: Traditional Bedouin**
>
> FBD (father's brother's daughter) preferred:
> - Man has first right to father's brother's daughter
> - Keeps bride price in family
> - Strengthens patrilineal solidarity
> - Though other marriages also occur

**Why Different Patterns?**
Cultural logic varies:
- Strengthen internal lineage (parallel allowed/preferred)
- Create external alliances (cross-cousin preferred)

## Exchange and Marriage Payments

### Marriage Isn't "Free"

**In most societies:** Marriage involves **exchange** of goods, services, people.

**Why?**
- Compensates for loss of labor
- Symbolizes value of relationship
- Creates ongoing obligations
- Binds families together

### Bridewealth (Brideprice)

**Definition:** Groom's family pays goods to bride's family.

**Common In:**
- Sub-Saharan Africa (cattle, money)
- Pastoral societies (livestock)
- Many parts of Asia

**What's Exchanged:**
- Livestock (cattle, camels)
- Money
- Goods (cloth, tools)
- Labor (bride service)

**Functions:**

**1. Compensation:**
- Bride's family loses daughter's labor
- Bridewealth compensates

**2. Legitimacy:**
- Children belong to group that paid bridewealth
- Establishes paternity rights
- Determines children's lineage membership

**3. Alliance:**
- Creates debt/obligation
- Ties families together
- Ongoing reciprocity

**4. Honor:**
- High bridewealth = high value of woman
- Shows respect for bride's family

**Not "Buying Women":**
- Women not property
- Bridewealth creates relationship, not purchase
- Families bound in reciprocal obligation

> **Example: Nuer Cattle Bridewealth**
>
> Traditional bridewealth = 40+ cattle:
> - Distributed among bride's maternal kin
> - Each recipient has rights/obligations to children
> - If marriage fails, cattle returned
> - Creates wide network of interested parties
> - Stabilizes marriage (many people invested)

**Modern Transformations:**
- Inflation (bridewealth too expensive)
- Commodification concerns (becoming like purchase)
- Women's rights debates
- Cash replacing cattle

### Dowry

**Definition:** Bride's family gives goods to bride (or groom's family).

**Common In:**
- India (historically)
- Mediterranean (historically)
- Some European societies (historically)

**What's Exchanged:**
- Money, jewelry, land
- Household goods
- Sometimes goes to bride (her security)
- Sometimes to groom's family

**Functions:**

**1. Provision for Daughter:**
- Her share of inheritance (if sons inherit family estate)
- Economic security in marriage

**2. Attracting Husband:**
- Especially in hypergamous systems (marry up in status)
- Compensate higher-status groom

**3. Status Display:**
- Wealth demonstration
- Family honor

> **Example: Traditional India**
>
> Dowry (dahez):
> - Bride's family gives to groom's family
> - Can include gold, cash, furniture, appliances
> - Sometimes enormous amounts
> - Created economic burden on families with daughters
>
> **Dowry abuse:**
> - Demands escalate
> - Bride harassment if insufficient
> - Dowry deaths (suspicious "kitchen fires")
>
> **Illegal since 1961** but practice persists informally.

**Problematic Aspects:**
- Economic burden on bride's family
- Potential for abuse
- Devalues daughters
- Modern movements against dowry

### Bride Service

**Definition:** Groom works for bride's family.

**Duration:** Months to years.

**Common In:**
- Some foraging societies
- Parts of South America
- Historically widespread

**Function:**
- Groom proves worthiness
- Compensates for loss of daughter's labor
- Builds relationship with in-laws

> **Example: Jacob in Genesis**
>
> Biblical account:
> - Jacob worked 7 years for Rachel
> - Then tricked, worked 7 more years
> - Bride service as payment
>
> Ancient Near Eastern practice reflected in story.

### Sister Exchange

**Definition:** Two families exchange daughters (no other payment).

**Creates:** Direct reciprocal alliance.

**If Works:** Balanced, fair.

**If Fails:** One family feels cheated, conflict.

## Alliance Theory (Lévi-Strauss)

### "Elementary Structures of Kinship" (1949)

**Claude Lévi-Strauss's Grand Theory:**

**Core Idea:** Marriage creates **alliance**, not just family.

**The Exchange of Women:**
- Men exchange women between groups
- Creates bonds between givers and receivers
- Fundamental to human society

**(Feminist critique: Why not "exchange of people"? Why assume men control? But theory influential despite gendered assumptions.)**

### Incest Taboo as Foundation

**Lévi-Strauss argued:**
- Incest taboo = UNIVERSAL
- Forces exogamy
- Exogamy = alliance
- Alliance = society

**"Marry out or die out":**
- Without alliances, groups conflict
- Marriage creates reciprocity
- Reciprocity creates social order

**Nature → Culture transition:**
Incest taboo transforms biological family into social alliances.

### Elementary vs. Complex Structures

**Elementary Structures:**
- **Positive marriage rules:** Prescribe WHO you should marry
- Cross-cousin marriage systems
- Creates systematic alliance networks
- Mathematically analyzable

**Complex Structures:**
- **Negative rules only:** Proscribe who you CAN'T marry
- But don't prescribe who you should
- Individual choice within prohibitions
- Western societies

**Lévi-Strauss studied:** Elementary structures (more patterned, reveal cultural logic).

### Types of Exchange

**Restricted Exchange:**
- Direct reciprocity: A ↔ B
- Symmetric, balanced
- FZD (patrilateral cross-cousin) marriage

**Generalized Exchange:**
- Circular: A → B → C → A
- Asymmetric
- MBD (matrilateral cross-cousin) marriage
- Creates wider alliances

**Lévi-Strauss preferred:** Generalized exchange (more complex, wider solidarity).

### Critiques of Alliance Theory

**1. Androcentric:**
Assumes men exchange women—what about women's agency?

**2. Overly Structural:**
Mathematical models miss messy reality.

**3. Western Projection:**
"Elementary" (non-Western) vs. "complex" (Western) hierarchy?

**4. Ignores Economics:**
Focuses on structure, not material conditions.

**5. Ahistorical:**
Synchronic analysis, no attention to change.

**But Influential:**
Shifted focus from descent to alliance, inspired decades of kinship research.

## Modern Marriage Transformations

### Love Marriage vs. Arranged Marriage

**Historical:** Most marriages arranged by families.

**Modern Shift:** "Love marriage" (individual choice) increasing globally.

**Arranged Marriage:**
- Families select partners
- Considerations: caste, class, wealth, alliances
- Courtship supervised
- Still common in parts of Asia, Middle East, Africa

**Love Marriage:**
- Individuals choose
- Romantic love primary
- Less family involvement
- Dominant in West, spreading

**Hybrid:** "Arranged introduction" (family introduces, couple decides).

**Debates:**
- Arranged marriage defenders: Practical, families know best, lower divorce
- Critics: Women's autonomy, forced marriage, abuse potential

**Reality:** Both forms have happy and unhappy marriages.

### Same-Sex Marriage

**Recent Legal Recognition:** (Many countries since 2000s)

**Challenges Traditional Definitions:**
- "Husband and wife" → "spouses"
- Gender roles?
- Procreation not central

**Anthropological Perspective:**
- Marriage has always varied
- Same-sex marriage just another variation
- Functions (alliance, economic, emotional) remain

**Kinship Systems Adapting:**
- How do terminologies work? (Two mothers? Two fathers?)
- Descent tracing?
- Extended families figuring it out

### New Reproductive Technologies

**Challenge Assumptions:**
- Sperm/egg donation
- Surrogacy
- IVF

**Questions:**
- Who is "mother"? (Genetic? Gestational? Social?)
- Who is "father"? (Genetic? Social?)
- Multiple "parents"?

**Kinship Becoming:**
- Less biological
- More social/intentional
- More complex

---

**Remember:** Marriage anthropologically isn't about romance—it's about alliance. Through marriage rules, societies create predictable patterns of cooperation between groups. Exogamy forces groups to ally; endogamy maintains group boundaries; cousin marriage creates systematic exchange; bridewealth and dowry make marriage a collective investment. Lévi-Strauss showed marriage organizes society structurally—though his theory is gendered and has limitations. Modern transformations (love marriage, same-sex marriage, new reproductive technologies) challenge traditional patterns, but marriage continues to create social bonds beyond the couple. Understanding marriage rules reveals how societies use kinship to create order, prevent conflict, and build solidarity.
