# Language Families and Linguistic Diversity: The Architecture of Human Communication

---

## The Diversity of Human Language

### How Many Languages?

**Current Count:**
- ~7,000 languages (Ethnologue)
- Constantly changing (births, deaths)
- Difficult to count (language vs. dialect)

**Distribution:**
- Highly uneven
- Papua New Guinea: ~840 languages (most diverse)
- Indonesia: ~700+
- Nigeria: ~500+
- India: ~780+

**Speakers:**
- Top 10 languages: ~40% of world population
  - Mandarin Chinese, Spanish, English, Hindi, Arabic, Bengali, Portuguese, Russian, Japanese, Punjabi
- Median language: ~8,000 speakers
- Many endangered (few speakers, elderly)

## What Is a Language Family?

### Genetic Relationships

**Definition:**
- Languages descended from common ancestor
- "Proto-language" (reconstructed)
- Diverged over time
- Share systematic correspondences

**Not:**
- Borrowing (loanwords)
- Contact (bilingualism, creoles separate issue)
- Typological similarity (coincidental)

**Family Tree Model:**
- Branches (subgroups)
- Leaves (individual languages)
- Criticism: Oversimplifies (contact, variation)

## Major Language Families

### Indo-European

**Largest Family (Speakers):**
- 3+ billion speakers
- 445+ languages
- Europe, South Asia, Americas (colonization)

**Branches:**

**Germanic:**
- English, German, Dutch, Swedish, Norwegian, Danish, Icelandic

**Romance:**
- Spanish, French, Italian, Portuguese, Romanian
- From Latin (Vulgar Latin)

**Slavic:**
- Russian, Polish, Czech, Bulgarian, Serbo-Croatian

**Indo-Iranian:**
- Hindi, Urdu, Bengali, Punjabi, Persian, Kurdish
- Most speakers (Indo-European)

**Celtic:**
- Irish, Welsh, Scots Gaelic, Breton
- Endangered (mostly)

**Greek, Albanian, Armenian:**
- Separate branches (each)

**Discovery:**
- Sir William Jones (1786)
- Sanskrit, Greek, Latin similarities
- "Common source" (Proto-Indo-European)

**Proto-Indo-European (PIE):**
- Reconstructed (never written)
- ~4,500-2,500 BCE (debated)
- Homeland debate:
  - Steppe hypothesis (Kurgan, Marija Gimbutas)
  - Anatolian hypothesis (Colin Renfrew)
  - Recent: Ancient DNA supports Steppe

- Cannot be classified into any family
- Unique

**Examples:**

**Basque:**
- Spain, France (Pyrenees)
- Pre-Indo-European (Europe)
- Survived

**Ainu:**
- Japan (Hokkaido)
- Nearly extinct

**Burushaski:**
- Pakistan (Hunza Valley)

**Sumerian:**
- Ancient Mesopotamia (extinct)
- Not Semitic

**Korean? Japanese?**

- Relatives extinct

## Phonology: Sound Systems

### Consonants and Vowels

**Inventory Size:**
- Rotokas (Papua New Guinea): 11 phonemes (smallest)
- !Xóõ (Botswana): 100+ phonemes (largest)

**Click Consonants:**
- Khoisan languages (Southern Africa)
- ǃKung, Nama, Sandawe
- Also: Xhosa, Zulu (borrowed from Khoisan)

**Ejectives:**
- Glottalized consonants
- Georgian, Amharic, Quechua, Haida

**Tones:**
- Pitch distinctions (lexical)
- **Mandarin:** 4 tones (mā, má, mǎ, mà = different meanings)
- **Cantonese:** 6-9 tones
- **Yoruba, Igbo:** Tonal
- **Thai, Vietnamese:** Tonal
- ~60-70% of languages (some tonality)

**Vowel Harmony:**
- Vowels agree (front/back, round/unround)
- Turkish, Finnish, Hungarian

## Morphology: Word Structure

### Types

**Isolating (Analytic):**
- One morpheme per word
- Little inflection
- Mandarin, Vietnamese
- Example (Mandarin): "我 看 了 书" (I see PAST book)
  - Each word = separate morpheme

**Agglutinative:**
- Multiple morphemes per word
- Clear boundaries
- Turkish, Finnish, Japanese, Swahili
- Example (Turkish): *evlerimizden* (ev-ler-imiz-den = house-PLURAL-our-FROM)
  - "from our houses"

**Fusional (Inflectional):**
- Multiple morphemes fuse
- Boundaries unclear
- Latin, Russian, German
- Example (Latin): *amō* (love-1SG.PRESENT)
  - One ending = person + number + tense

**Polysynthetic:**
- Many morphemes per word
- Whole sentences in one word
- Inuktitut, Mohawk, Greenlandic
- Example (Greenlandic): *tusaanngitsuusaartuaannarsinnaavara*
  - "I simply cannot hear it"
  - One word!

**Spectrum:**
- Not absolute categories
- Languages mix types

## Syntax: Sentence Structure

### Word Order

**Basic Order (Subject-Verb-Object):**

**SOV (Subject-Object-Verb):**
- **Most common** (~45%)
- Japanese, Turkish, Korean, Hindi, Persian

**SVO (Subject-Verb-Object):**
- **Second most common** (~42%)
- English, Mandarin, French, Russian, Swahili

**VSO (Verb-Subject-Object):**
- ~9%
- Irish, Welsh, Arabic, Tagalog

**Others (Rare):**
- VOS, OVS, OSV
- ~5% combined

**Free Word Order:**
- Some languages (case marking)
- Latin, Russian (flexible)

## Implicational Universals

### Patterns

**Greenberg's Universals (1963):**
- Cross-linguistic tendencies
- If X, then (usually) Y

**Examples:**

**Universal 17:**
- VSO languages: prepositions (not postpositions)
- SOV languages: postpositions (not prepositions)

**Universal 2:**
- In declarative sentences with nominal subject and object, the dominant order is almost always one in which the subject precedes the object.

**Universal 45:**
- If there are both prepositions and postpositions, the language is SOV.

**Not Absolute:**
- Tendencies, not rules
