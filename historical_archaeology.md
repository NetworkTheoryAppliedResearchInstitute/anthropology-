# Historical Archaeology: Studying the Recent Past

---

## What Is Historical Archaeology?

### Definition and Scope

**Historical Archaeology:**
- Study of societies with written records
- **But:** archaeology still essential
  - Material culture complements documents
  - Fills gaps (non-literate groups)
  - Challenges official narratives
  - Everyday life (not in texts)

**Time Depth:**
- Varies by region
- Europe: Medieval onwards (~500 CE+)
- Americas: Post-contact (1492+)
- Africa, Asia: Variable

**"Text-Aided Archaeology":**
- Documents + material culture
- Synergy

---

## Why Historical Archaeology?

### What Can It Tell Us?

**Documents Are Biased:**
- Elite perspective
- Literate minority
- Official versions
- Silence: women, enslaved, poor, indigenous

**Archaeology Reveals:**
- Material reality (what people actually did)
- Subaltern voices
- Contradictions
- Daily life

**Example:**
- Documents: "Slaves lived well"
- Archaeology: Poor nutrition (skeletal evidence), cramped quarters, limited possessions

**Focus:**

- Cultural contact
- Indigenous responses
- Material culture of colonialism

## Spanish Colonialism (Americas)

### Missions

**California Missions (~1769-1834):**
- Franciscan
- Convert Native Americans
- 21 missions (San Diego to Sonoma)

**Archaeology:**
- Native quarters vs. Spanish
- Differential access to goods
- Disease (skeletal evidence)
- Persistence of indigenous practices
  - Traditional crafts
  - Burial customs

**Critique of "Peaceful Conversion":**
- Forced labor
- High mortality
- Cultural suppression
- Resistance (rebellion, flight)

### St. Augustine (Florida, 1565)

**Oldest Continuous European Settlement (US):**
- Spanish colony
- Kathleen Deagan (excavator)

**Findings:**
- Mestizaje (cultural mixing)
  - Spanish men, indigenous/African women
  - Households blend Spanish/indigenous
  - Pottery: indigenous forms, Spanish decorations
- Gender: women maintain indigenous foodways

## United States: Antebellum South

### Thomas Jefferson's Monticello

**Paradox:**
- "All men are created equal"
- Enslaved 600+ people

**Archaeology:**
- Mulberry Row (enslaved quarters)
- Sally Hemings (DNA evidence)
- Nailery (children's labor)

**Findings:**
- Possessions (limited but personalized)
- Crafts, gardening (subsistence)
- African-influenced pottery
- BaKongo cosmology (artifacts)

### Plantation Sites (General)

**Material Culture:**
- Ceramics (colonoware, African traditions)
- Beads (personal adornment, religion)
- Subfloor pits (storage, ritual?)

**Skeletal Evidence:**
- Malnutrition
- Disease
- Trauma (violence, labor)
- Infant mortality

**Resistance:**
- Maroon communities (runaways)
- Palmares (Brazil, 1600s)
  - Quilombo (settlement)
  - Thousands of people
  - Resisted Portuguese

## World War Archaeology

### Battlefields

**Custer's Last Stand (Little Bighorn, 1876):**
- Battle (US Cavalry vs. Lakota, Cheyenne)
- Richard Fox (archaeologist)
- Metal detectors (bullets, shells)

**Findings:**
- Native tactics (archaeology contradicts myths)
- Custer's errors

### Conflict Archaeology

**World Wars:**
- Trenches (WWI)
- Camps, battlefields (WWII)

**Example: Sobibor (Poland):**
- Nazi death camp
- Archaeology reveals layout
- Evidence of uprising (1943)

## Archaeology of Consumerism

### Garbage Archaeology

**William Rathje (Garbage Project, 1970s+):**
- Tucson, Arizona
- Landfill excavations

**Findings:**
- What people throw away ≠ what they say
- Food waste (significant)
- Hazardous materials
- Consumption patterns

**Lessons:**
- Discard behavior
- Environmental impact
- Policy (recycling)
